# Renewing the Long Experiment
### National Experiential Civic Education Framework (NECEF)

*A national policy campaign to embed hands-on civic education in every U.S. school.*

---

## The Problem

American democracy depends on citizens who know how to cooperate, serve, and lead. That capacity is declining. Civic knowledge is deeply unstable — dropping to 32% of Americans able to name all three branches of government in 2018, fluctuating to 47% in 2022, shifting again with each news cycle (Annenberg Public Policy Center). Volunteerism is down. Social trust is eroding. A generation is entering adulthood without the practiced habits of self-government.

This is not a cultural curiosity. It is an active threat to the stability and functionality of democratic governance.

---

## The Proposal

The **National Experiential Civic Education Framework (NECEF)** embeds structured, hands-on civic programs in schools nationwide — giving students real practice in the habits democracy requires.

**Four core pillars:**

1. **Community Service Projects** — Structured, recurring service addressing real local needs. 40-hour annual minimum as a pilot entry point, with states expected to scale toward higher thresholds as programs mature.
2. **Emergency Preparedness Training** — First aid, CPR, disaster response coordination, and community resilience planning, built directly on the existing FEMA CERT program.
3. **Infrastructure and Community Stewardship** — Students participate in local infrastructure and community improvement projects, locally defined and politically neutral.
4. **Leadership Rotations and Collaborative Challenges** — Structured team-based problem-solving modeled on the Project Lead The Way framework, emphasizing listening, negotiation, and coordination across difference.

**Cost:** $57–$87 per student per year.  
**Bureaucracy:** None new. Builds on AmeriCorps, FEMA, and Title IV — existing federal infrastructure.  
**Control:** Full state and local control over program design and content.

---

## Why This Works

NECEF is not untested theory. It is a national scaling of programs with decades of documented outcomes:

- **City Year** (est. 1988) — AmeriCorps-funded program operating in 29 U.S. cities with 35+ years of documented civic outcomes.
- **Maryland Service Graduation Requirement** (est. 1992) — The first state to require 75 hours of community service for graduation. Studies show measurably higher rates of volunteering and civic participation in adulthood among Maryland graduates.
- **FEMA CERT** — Already operating in thousands of schools and communities nationwide. Communities with CERT-trained residents demonstrate measurably faster disaster response.
- **Project Lead The Way** — Applied, project-based curricula operating in 12,200+ schools across all 50 states with 116,000+ trained teachers, proving rigorous applied programming is implementable at national scale.

## Historical Grounding

The idea that America is an ongoing experiment in democratic self-government runs through its foundational texts and most perceptive observers.

The Constitution's opening — *We the People* — located sovereignty in the citizenry itself. Madison argued in Federalist No. 51 that no constitutional structure could substitute for civic virtue. Tocqueville identified voluntary civic association as the engine of American democratic vitality and warned that its loss would hollow out the republic from within. Putnam's *Bowling Alone* (2000) provided the empirical proof: where civic engagement falls, democratic health follows.

Each arrived at the same conclusion: democracy is not self-sustaining. It must be actively cultivated, generation by generation.

The republic was always a wager on the capability of its citizens. Every generation must choose to tend it.

## Who Needs to Act

- **Federal policymakers** — Framework legislation authorizing NECEF and its associated Title IV and AmeriCorps funding mechanisms
- **Governors and state education leaders** — State-level adoption drawing on federal partnership funding
- **Philanthropic foundations** — Co-fund the Phase 1 pilot and independent outcome research
- **Civic and nonprofit organizations** — Partner as program delivery providers and community liaisons
- **Business leaders** — Advocate through the Chamber; co-sponsor school programs in operating communities
